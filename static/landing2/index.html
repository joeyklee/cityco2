At the Urban Climate Group at the University of British Columbia we are currently focused on trying to understand the complex interplay between the social and physical dimensions of cities that result in their increased emissions.We are particularly interested to map the spatial and temporal dimensions of emissions so that cities - their governments, communities and residents - can begin to find ways to reduce emissions and build more sustainable and liveable cities.

Why Cities

Nearly 70% of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions worldwide have been attributed to cities. While cities are a major contributor to increasing GHG concentrations in the earth's atmosphere - and thus climate change - they also have the greatest potential to reduce their carbon footprint.

Through policy interventions, urban design, community participation, and behavioral change, cities hold the key to a more sustainable future.

Status Quo

However, the current tools monitoring our GHG emissions can not give us feedback at the spatial scales we can relate to and plan around. Traditional methods operate at coarse spatial resolutions (e.g. measured from space over thousands of kilometers) or at sparsely located fixed points.

How can we as communities and individuals begin to make more conscious decisions about reducing our emissions if we dont have the tools to take ownership of our emissions?

Building A Smarter City

Building Mobile Sensors

Given improvements in technology and the growth of the opensource community, new opportunities exist to help benchmarch our emissions in our communities. Using a mobile approach to sensing and monitoring emissions, we can use cars and bikes to measure greenhouse gas concentrations at a local level/neighborhood level.

Inspired by opensourced technologies, we are using Arduino to power our CO2 sensor system. The versatility of the Arduino has allowed us to plug-and-play the Adafruit GPS shield (latitude, longitude, vehicle speed, and elevation) with a LiCor CO2 Infrared Gas Analyzer, as well as a XYZ digital thermometer.
